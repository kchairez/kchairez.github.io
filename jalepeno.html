Jalapeño

Chile Pepper

---Add some spice to your diet with jalapeño. Named for the town of Jalapa, Mexico, this is the most popular chile pepper in the United States. Jalapeño produces 3-inch, thick-walled, moderately hot pods with deep green color that matures to a bright red. The skin may show a netting pattern as fruit ages, but it does not affect flavor. Often, the heat of the peppers will vary, even those from the same plant. If peppers grow fast, get plenty of water, and are harvested soon, they may be milder than peppers that stay on the plant a long time, or that develop slowly and under stressful conditions. Widely adapted, jalapeño plants yield a bountiful harvest in dry or humid, hot or cool climates. This makes jalapeños an easy grow. The compact plants grow well in containers and can be easily purchased already partially grown in containers for easy planting.

Season: Spring, Summer

Light: Full Sun

Plant Spacing: 18-24"

Matures: At least 72 Days

Scoville Heat: 2500-8000

  • Planting begins late spring/early summer. Ensure that soil temps have reached +70°F or wait 2 weeks after the last frost of the season in the area. Plant in a sunny, wind safe area.
  • Space individual seedlings/pots in the soil 1.5'- 2' apart in nutrient-rich, well drained soil (they also can tolerate sandy soils with moderate nutrients). They can also be planted indoors with high sun exposure in large pots with added soil components to prevent soil compaction.
  • Give the jalapeños a headstart by providing a few inches of of aged compost or other decomposing organic matter.
  • Be sure to apply 1-2 inches of water per week, allow deep watering to prevent shallow roots. Drip irrigation is recommended but water in the morning to prevent foliar disease. Adding a mulch layer helps with soil moisture but ensure warm soil temps.
  • Jalapeños benefit from regular fertilizer, so, tomato and garden fertilizer is recommended without over fertilizing.
  • Be sure to consistently weed without disturbing the roots.
  • Jalapeños are typically harvested in the green, immature phase but you can allow them to ripen to red, which typically get hotter the longer they are on the plant. More info
